☎ 143 · Crisis

Your threat response fired. Cortisol and adrenaline are now physically in your bloodstream. The body reacts before thought. Start with the biological interventions — they change your brain chemistry directly. Psychological tools work after the physiology settles.

⚡ Under 60 seconds — biological first strike do these first

💨
Physiological sigh × 3
Double inhale through nose (sniff, then sniff more), then long exhale through mouth. Repeat 3×.
↳ Fastest known breathing method to drop cortisol in real time (Huberman Lab, Stanford). Deflates air sacs, activates vagus nerve immediately.
✅
🥶
Cold water on face or wrists — 30 seconds
Run cold water over your face and wrists, or press a cold glass against your cheek.
↳ Triggers the mammalian dive reflex. Drops heart rate 10-25% in under 30 seconds. No willpower required — it's pure physiology.
✅
🦶
Press feet hard into the floor — 10 seconds
Both feet flat. Push down hard. Feel the resistance. This is not a metaphor.
↳ Proprioceptive input (pressure/joint position) is processed in the same thalamic circuit that gates the amygdala alarm. Physical grounding has a measurable dampening effect on threat response.
✅

⏱ 2–5 minutes — bring cortisol down if you can

🏃
Move your body — 50 jumping jacks, run in place, walk fast
Any vigorous movement for 2-3 minutes.
↳ Cortisol and adrenaline are designed to fuel movement. Burning them through exercise is the most direct metabolic pathway. Standing still with cortisol in your blood is what makes the feeling unbearable.
✅
🍌
Eat something — anything with protein or fat
Even a few bites. A handful of nuts, bread, fruit, anything.
↳ Cortisol is a stress hormone AND a blood-sugar hormone. Low blood sugar triggers cortisol release as a survival mechanism. If you haven't eaten recently, you may be in a cortisol loop that food can break.
✅
🫂
Move toward a calm person — same room is enough
Your WG group, a café, anywhere humans are present and not hostile.
↳ Co-regulation is biological. The ventral vagal system (social engagement) is inhibited during threat response. A calm nervous system nearby literally entrains yours — cardiac synchrony, cortisol drop via oxytocin.
✅

🧠 Once your body starts to settle then, not before

🏷
Name the feeling — one word
Say it silently: "I feel angry." / "I feel humiliated." / "I feel scared." Not the story — just the emotion word.
↳ Affect labeling (naming emotions) reduces amygdala activation and increases prefrontal regulation in fMRI studies (Lieberman 2007). The act of finding a word brings cortex back online. This is not therapy — it's neuroscience.
✅
⏸
Don't respond yet — to anything
Whatever the situation is, it can wait 10 minutes. You have the right to not respond immediately.
↳ Decisions and words made in high cortisol states tend to escalate, not resolve. The "refractory period" after a trigger is when most additional damage happens. Waiting is not weakness — it's prefrontal strategy.
✅
🌊
The thought spiral — interrupt it now
If worst-case thoughts are looping, go to the 💭 Thoughts tab. That's what it's for.
↳ Catastrophizing loops amplify cortisol further. The spiral isn't truth — it's the amygdala's threat model running without a brake. It needs to be interrupted structurally, not suppressed.

Physiological sigh: Double inhale (sniff · sniff) then long exhale. Stanford research shows this is the fastest way to reduce physiological arousal in real time — works in under 3 breaths. It works by deflating air sacs (alveoli) that have collapsed during stress breathing, which restores the CO₂/O₂ balance that drives the calm signal.
When you're triggered, the amygdala generates threat predictions — not facts. Worst-case scenarios feel certain. The brain's negativity bias and pattern-matching create a loop. This tool breaks that loop structurally — not by forcing positive thinking, but by separating what happened from what your brain is adding.
Step 1 · What distortion is this?
Which of these best describes what the thought is doing?
Step 2 · What actually happened?
Facts only — no interpretation. What literally occurred?
Step 3 · What am I adding?
What meaning, prediction or judgment am I layering on top of those facts?
Step 4 · How likely is the worst case?
On a scale 1–10, how likely is the absolute worst outcome?
Step 5 · The realistic middle
Not the best case. Not the worst. What's the most likely actual outcome?
Step 6 · What would you tell a close friend?
If your best friend had this exact thought about this exact situation — what would you say to them?

Your reframe

5-4-3-2-1 Grounding forces your brain into present-moment sensory processing, interrupting the amygdala's threat narrative. Each sense adds a separate channel of "safe present data" — the more channels, the harder it is for the fear circuit to maintain the alarm.
👀 See · 5 things
5 things you can see right now
Name 5 actual objects in your physical space. Be specific — not "a chair" but "the green plastic chair with the scratch."
✋ Touch · 4 things
4 things you can physically feel
Touch your surroundings. Temperature, texture, pressure, weight.
👂 Hear · 3 things
3 things you can hear
Listen for background sounds you usually filter — traffic, airflow, your own heartbeat.
👃 Smell · 2 things
2 things you can smell
Smell is processed directly by the limbic system — it's one of the most powerful grounding channels.
👅 Taste · 1 thing
1 thing you can taste
Any taste. Take a sip of water if you have some — the cold or warmth is data too.
